# John's Writing Style Profile

John's natural voice extracted from his handwritten essays. The audit skill should preserve and amplify these patterns, not flatten them.

---

## Voice

- **Raw-first.** Spits out thoughts unfiltered, then shapes them. The rawness is the point.
  - "I've been mass producing the wrong things."
  - "We were building fast and not talking to the people using what we built."
- **First-person dominant.** "I", "We", "My co-founder and I." Never hides behind passive or third-person.
  - "I mass-produced 21 versions of onboarding"
  - "Yujong and I would talk about it"
- **Opinionated without disclaimers.** States positions directly. No hedging.
  - "If your product exists solely to solve a narrow, one-off task, it's not a business anymore. It's a prompt."
  - "Everyone should think about their tombstone more often."
- **Self-critical.** Admits mistakes openly. No spin, no softening.
  - "That was wrong."
  - "All five are the same mistake."
  - "I was breaking it without realizing."
- **Conversational authority.** Writes like explaining to a peer. Dashes, parentheticals, asides.
  - "The real danger — the one no one talks about — is the slow bleed."
  - "It sounds dramatic (it kind of is)."
- **Compact, not lossy.** No verbosity, no redundancy — but never cuts substance. Repetition only when it's intentional emphasis.

## Structure

- **Hook first.** Opens with a provocative claim or personal observation. Never an abstract setup.
  - "The 'SaaS-pocalypse' narrative is right. It's just pointing at the wrong companies."
  - "Post-YC slump is something I'm trying to avoid at all costs."
  - "Everyone should think about their tombstone more often."
- **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences typical. Single-sentence paragraphs are common and intentional.
  - "Ship what matters. Kill everything else." (full paragraph)
  - "That's the hard part." (standalone line after a longer explanation)
- **Horizontal rules (`---`) as section dividers.** Functional breaks between distinct ideas, not decorative.
- **Bold headers for sections** — punchy, not academic.
  - "**The real threat**" not "**An Analysis of Emerging Competitive Dynamics**"
- **Ends with specific takeaway or unresolved tension.** Not "the future looks bright."
  - "Good software always prevails. That part hasn't changed."
  - "Saying no to protect what matters is harder, but also far more valuable."
  - "I'm not going to start shipping slowly. But I am going to communicate before I break things."

## Sentence Patterns

- **Varied length.** 4-word fragments next to 30-word explanations. Never metronomic.
  - "That's gone." → then a full explanatory sentence.
  - "All of those looked like progress. They weren't."
- **Declarative-dominant.** Statements, not questions. Questions only when genuinely asking.
  - "So what do you want them to say?"
  - "What would you want on yours?"
- **Contrarian framing.** Sets up the common take, then pivots.
  - "AI isn't killing SaaS. It's killing *replaceable* SaaS."
  - "Acting fast doesn't always mean moving forward."
- **Fragments for emphasis.** Not afraid to break grammar rules for rhythm.
  - "Not because they're bad, but because they were built before the founders understood the problem."

## Evidence Style

- **Personal experience as primary evidence.**
  - "Char had been asking for this for weeks"
  - "Yujong and I would talk about it after every sprint"
- **Named sources with links.** Cited with URLs, not vague attribution.
  - "a16z's report showed..." / "Sam Altman said..."
- **Real numbers.**
  - "21 versions," "200 daily active users churned," "18% between 2022 and 2024"
- **User quotes when relevant.** Direct quotes, italicized, unedited.
  - *"I just want it to work without thinking about it"*

## Tone Spectrum

- **Blog essays (published):** Conversational-professional. Data-backed contrarian takes.
  - "AI isn't killing SaaS. It's killing *replaceable* SaaS."
- **Postmortems:** Unflinching self-assessment.
  - "All five are the same mistake."
- **Short reflections:** Punchy, philosophical, personal. More conviction than citations.
  - "Everyone should think about their tombstone more often."
- **Drafts/outlines:** Bullet-point thinking, stream-of-consciousness.
- **Korean essays:** Emotionally raw, repetitive for emphasis.

## Anti-patterns (things John does NOT do)

- No bloat — no redundant transitions or padding. But never sacrifice information for brevity. Deliberate repetition for emphasis is fine.
- No hedging language ("it could be argued," "one might suggest")
- No significance inflation ("marks a pivotal moment," "underscores the importance")
- No generic conclusions ("the future looks bright," "exciting times ahead")
- No academic distance ("it can be observed that")
- No promotional adjectives ("groundbreaking," "revolutionary")
- No emoji-heavy formatting
- No three-item rhetorical lists for emphasis
- Rarely uses "So" to start paragraphs
- Never uses filler phrases ("Here's the thing:", "Let me be clear")

## Rhythm Signature

John's rhythm alternates between **punch and explain**:
1. Short declarative statement (the point)
2. Longer sentence or two (the evidence/context)
3. Short closer (the landing)
